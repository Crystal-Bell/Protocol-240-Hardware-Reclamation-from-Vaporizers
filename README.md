Most vape liquids are composed of a few standard ingredients:
​Propylene Glycol (PG): A synthetic, colorless, odorless liquid used as a solvent and humectant.  
​Vegetable Glycerin (VG): A thicker, natural compound derived from vegetable oils.  
​Additives: Nicotine, flavor concentrates, and sometimes trace metals or volatile organic compounds (VOCs).  
​Is it a Fire Starter? (The "Combustion Protocol")
​While you might think of it as "fuel," e-liquid is technically a Class IIIB combustible liquid.  
​The Flash Point: PG has a flash point of about 210°F (99°C), and VG is even higher at 320°F (160°C). This means you cannot ignite it with a simple spark or match like you would with gasoline.  
​The Verdict: It isn't an efficient fire starter. If you poured it on wood, it would likely just smoke and turn into a sticky, messy residue rather than catching flame easily.  
​Safety Warning: Never attempt to burn nicotine-containing liquids in a way that could release the vapor into your immediate breathing space. Heating nicotine can change its chemical state, and inhaling concentrated aerosols or combustion byproducts is hazardous.  
​The True Utility: Modular Components
​Rather than focusing on the liquid, the "Architect" looks at the hardware you've already identified. You are correct that these devices are gold mines for modular parts:
​The Batteries (The Power Source): As you noted, these hold a charge and have leads. They are excellent for low-current projects, LED lighting, or even field-expedient heating (like shorting a wire to create a red-hot ignition point for char cloth).  
​The Magnets (The Structural Grid): The small, high-strength magnets from pod-based systems are perfect for the Wall-Mounted Inventory System we discussed. They can be epoxied to your aluminum "vanity" cans to hold them securely to a magnetic strip or board.
​The Casings: These are often high-quality plastics or metals that can be cleaned and repurposed as small-part storage for your "sifter" drawer system.
​Architect’s Note on Waste
​If you are clearing out these devices, do not dump the liquid into the soil or water. Even though PG and VG are "food safe" in small, regulated amounts, the concentrated nicotine and trace heavy metals found in the remaining liquid are environmental contaminants. The best path is to extract the magnets and battery (for your workshop) and dispose of the remaining liquid and casing according to local hazardous waste/battery recycling guidelines
